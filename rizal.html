La obra de la vida de Rizal

José Rizal's Character Development

Introduction. José Protasio Rizal (1861–1896), a scholar, physician, novelist, and nationalist, stands as the acknowledged national hero of the Philippines (Ocampo, 2011). His privileged status as "outstanding Filipino author results from the ease with which he wrote and his essential contributions to the birth of the nation" (Ocampo, 2011, p. 1). Guerrero (1963) famously described him as "The First Filipino"—a man whose consciousness of national identity preceded that of his contemporaries.

Thesis Statement. José Rizal's character and heroism were shaped by a confluence of biological predispositions—including his inherited intellectual aptitude and multi-ethnic ancestry (Panlasagui, 1999)—and profound environmental influences, such as his privileged family upbringing (Guerrero, 1963), world-class education (Ocampo, 2011), the oppressive socio-political climate of colonial Philippines (Constantino, 1971), and his transformative experiences abroad (Quibuyen, 1997). These intersecting forces forged a man whose virtues, sacrifices, and ultimate martyrdom ignited the flames of Philippine independence.

I

Biological Factors

The inherited traits and physical characteristics that shaped Rizal's identity

🧬

Inherited Traits

  • From his father, Francisco Mercado: Rizal inherited independence, self-reliance, and quiet determination. Guerrero (1963) records Rizal calling his father "a model of fathers," an industrious farmer from Biñan, Laguna.
  • From his mother, Teodora Alonso: He inherited exceptional intellectual ability and literary talent. Guerrero (1963) described Doña Teodora as "a highly cultured and accomplished woman" whom Rizal called a "loving and prudent mother." At age three, Rizal learned the alphabet from her, and by five, he "already showed inclinations to be an artist" (Guerrero, 1963, p. 1).
  • Chinese ancestry: Panlasagui (1999) traced Rizal's lineage to the Chinese immigrant Domingo Lam-co, who settled in the Philippines in the late 17th century. Panlasagui (1999) concluded that "the most significant Chinese contribution to the Filipino people is not in the form of economic goods and skills, but in the form of human beings—the large number of Chinese who came and settled in the Philippines."
  • Malay heritage: From both parental lines, Rizal inherited his love for freedom and deep connection to the Filipino people. Panlasagui (1999) argues that "the Great Malayan's lineage shows that the Philippines is the real melting pot of races, the dream of oneness of mankind come true."
  • Spanish blood: Through the Lakandula lineage and other aristocratic connections, giving Rizal an understanding of both colonized and colonizer perspectives (Agoncillo & Guerrero, 1979).
👤

Physical Characteristics & Their Impact

  • Small stature: Rizal's below-average height reportedly motivated him to excel intellectually and physically to compensate, leading him to become proficient in fencing, marksmanship, and gymnastics (Guerrero, 1963; Ocampo, 2011).
  • Weak eyesight: His nearsightedness contributed to extensive reading habits and later influenced his decision to study ophthalmology—motivated in part by his desire to treat his mother's failing eyes (Guerrero, 1963).
  • Prominent forehead: Often noted by contemporaries as indicating intellectual capacity, contributing to his scholarly appearance and the "ilustrado" image he embodied (Ocampo, 1990).
  • Physical frailty in youth: Guerrero (1963) describes how the young Rizal was somewhat sickly, developing inner strength and determination that pushed him to overcome physical limitations through discipline.
  • Charismatic presence: Despite not being conventionally handsome, his intelligence, eloquence, and magnetic personality attracted people to him wherever he traveled in Europe and Asia (Guerrero, 1963; Quibuyen, 1997).
II

Environmental Factors

The family, education, and socio-political context that molded Rizal's worldview

Calamba, Laguna

Rizal's birthplace — a prosperous agricultural town

Foundation of Learning

The Mercado-Alonso household prioritized education

The Mercado-Alonso Family

José Rizal was born on June 19, 1861, in Calamba, Laguna, to Francisco Mercado and Teodora Alonso Realonda, "the seventh child in a family of 11 children (2 boys and 9 girls)" (Guerrero, 1963, p. 1). Both parents "were educated and belonged to distinguished families" (Guerrero, 1963, p. 1).


  • Wealthy landowners: The family leased productive farmlands from the Dominican Order, providing José with a comfortable upbringing and access to quality education (Agoncillo & Guerrero, 1979).
  • Home library: The Mercado household had one of the largest private libraries in Calamba, exposing young José to a wide range of literature (Ocampo, 1990).
  • Mother as first teacher: Doña Teodora taught José the alphabet at age three (Guerrero, 1963). Her storytelling nurtured his imagination and love for literature.
  • Religious but critical: While devoutly Catholic, the family witnessed abuses by the friars, planting early seeds of awareness about colonial injustice (Constantino, 1971).
  • Siblings: Growing up among ten siblings—Saturnina, Paciano, Narcisa, Olimpia, Lucia, Maria, José, Concepción, Josefa, Trinidad, Soledad—taught him social skills, empathy, and family loyalty (Guerrero, 1963).

Early Education (Calamba & Biñan)

  • Age 3 (1864): "At the age of 3, he learned the alphabet from his mother" (Guerrero, 1963, p. 1).
  • Age 5: "While learning to read and write, he already showed inclinations to be an artist. He astounded his family and relatives by his pencil drawings and sketches and by his moldings of clay" (Guerrero, 1963, p. 1).
  • Age 8 (1869): "He wrote a Tagalog poem, 'Sa Aking Mga Kabata,' the theme of which revolves on the love of one's language" (Guerrero, 1963, p. 1).
  • 1869: Sent to Biñan to study under Maestro Justiniano Aquino Cruz, where he excelled academically (Guerrero, 1963; Ocampo, 2011).

Ateneo Municipal de Manila (1872–1877)

  • "In 1877, at the age of 16, he obtained his Bachelor of Arts degree with an average of 'excellent' from the Ateneo Municipal de Manila" (Guerrero, 1963, p. 1).
  • Won five medals upon graduation and developed literary talents through poetry competitions (Ocampo, 2011).
  • The Jesuit education instilled discipline and intellectual rigor that would define his later scholarship (Ocampo, 2011).

Universidad de Santo Tomás (1877–1882)

  • "In the same year [1877], he enrolled in Philosophy and Letters at the University of Santo Tomas, while at the same time took courses leading to the degree of surveyor and expert assessor at the Ateneo" (Guerrero, 1963, p. 1).
  • "He finished the latter course on March 21, 1877 and passed the Surveyor's examination on May 21, 1878" (Guerrero, 1963, p. 1).
  • "In 1878, he enrolled in medicine at the University of Santo Tomas but had to stop in his studies when he felt that the Filipino students were being discriminated upon by their Dominican tutors" (Guerrero, 1963, p. 1).

Universidad Central de Madrid (1882–1885)

  • Earned Licentiate in Medicine (1884) and Licentiate in Philosophy and Letters (1885) (Guerrero, 1963; Ocampo, 2011).
  • Studied painting, sculpture, and multiple languages at the Academy of Fine Arts of San Fernando (Quibuyen, 1997).
  • Became active in the Propaganda Movement, connecting with Filipino expatriate intellectuals (Ocampo, 2011).

The Colonial Philippines (1861–1896)

During Rizal's lifetime, the Philippines was under Spanish colonial rule for over 300 years. Constantino (1971) argues that understanding Rizal requires situating him within this oppressive colonial context, which "profoundly shaped his consciousness."


  • Friar domination: Spanish friars controlled vast haciendas, education, and local governance, often abusing their power (Agoncillo & Guerrero, 1979; Constantino, 1971). The Mercado family themselves experienced land disputes with the Dominicans.
  • Racial discrimination: Filipinos (called "Indios") were treated as second-class citizens despite their capabilities. Rizal personally experienced this at UST (Guerrero, 1963).
  • Lack of representation: The Philippines had no representation in the Spanish Cortes during much of the 19th century (Agoncillo & Guerrero, 1979).
  • Censorship and repression: Freedom of speech and press were severely limited; expressions of nationalism were punished harshly (Constantino, 1971).
  • The GOMBURZA execution (1872): When Rizal was 11, three Filipino priests were garroted on false charges. His brother Paciano was close to Father Burgos, and this event "deeply traumatized the family and radicalized young José" (Agoncillo & Guerrero, 1979; Guerrero, 1963).
  • Mother's imprisonment: In 1871, Doña Teodora was arrested on trumped-up charges and forced to walk 50 kilometers to prison, where she was held for over two years (Guerrero, 1963).
III

Interactive Timeline

Key events and milestones in José Rizal's life journey

June 19, 1861
Birth in Calamba, Laguna
"José Rizal, the national hero of the Philippines and pride of the Malayan race, was born on June 19, 1861, in the town of Calamba, Laguna" (Guerrero, 1963, p. 1). He was baptized three days later by Fr. Rufino Collantes at the Calamba church (Guerrero, 1963).
1864–1869
Early Education at Home
Taught by his mother Teodora, José learned the alphabet at age three (Guerrero, 1963). By age eight, he wrote the Tagalog poem "Sa Aking Mga Kabata," which "revolves on the love of one's language" (Guerrero, 1963, p. 1).
1869
Studies in Biñan
Sent to Biñan to study under Maestro Justiniano Aquino Cruz. Despite being small in stature, he excelled academically and defeated the maestro's son in a wrestling match, earning respect among his peers (Guerrero, 1963; Ocampo, 2011).
1872
GOMBURZA Execution & Entry to Ateneo
The execution of Fathers Gomez, Burgos, and Zamora shook the nation and the Rizal family (Agoncillo & Guerrero, 1979). "Paciano was devastated" (Guerrero, 1963). That same year, José enrolled at the Ateneo Municipal de Manila, beginning his remarkable academic career under the Jesuits (Guerrero, 1963).
1877
Ateneo Graduation & UST Enrollment
"In 1877, at the age of 16, he obtained his Bachelor of Arts degree with an average of 'excellent' from the Ateneo Municipal de Manila. In the same year, he enrolled in Philosophy and Letters at the University of Santo Tomas" (Guerrero, 1963, p. 1).
May 3, 1882
Departure for Spain
At age 20, Rizal left the Philippines secretly aboard the steamer Salvadora bound for Singapore, then onward to Europe. This marked "the beginning of his life abroad and his transformation into a reformist intellectual" (Quibuyen, 1997; Guerrero, 1963).
1882–1892
Travels Across Europe and Asia
Rizal traveled extensively to Spain, France, Germany, Austria, Switzerland, Italy, England, Belgium, Japan, China, and the United States (Quibuyen, 1997; Guerrero, 1963). He mastered over 22 languages, studied ophthalmology, and engaged with intellectuals and political movements across continents (Ocampo, 2011).
1887
Publication of Noli Me Tangere
"Published in Berlin, Germany, with financial help from friend Máximo Viola" (Ocampo, 2011, p. 1). The novel "articulates the abuse and the discrimination of the Spaniards towards the Filipinos" and "exposed the abuses of Spanish friars and colonial administration" (Ocampo, 2011, p. 2).
1891
Publication of El Filibusterismo
"El filibusterismo, published in 1891" (Ocampo, 2011, p. 1). This darker sequel explored themes of revolution and corruption, and was "dedicated to the memory of GOMBURZA" (Agoncillo & Guerrero, 1979).
1892
Founding of La Liga Filipina
Upon returning to Manila, Rizal founded La Liga Filipina on July 3, 1892—"a civic organization aimed at uniting Filipinos and promoting reform" (Agoncillo & Guerrero, 1979). Days later, he was arrested and exiled to Dapitan, Zamboanga (Quibuyen, 1997).
1892–1896
Exile in Dapitan
During his four-year exile, Rizal remained productive through "progressive education, social entrepreneurship, and community development" (Quibuyen, n.d.). He practiced medicine, established a school for boys, engaged in scientific research, and met Josephine Bracken, his common-law wife (Quibuyen, n.d.; Guerrero, 1963).
July 1896
Departure from Dapitan
Rizal volunteered as a military doctor in Cuba and was permitted to leave Dapitan (Guerrero, 1963). He was arrested en route and brought back to Manila, as the Philippine Revolution had broken out (Agoncillo & Guerrero, 1979; Quibuyen, 1997).
December 30, 1896
Execution at Bagumbayan
After a sham trial by military court, Rizal was convicted of rebellion, sedition, and illegal association (Guerrero, 1963). "At dawn on December 30, 1896, he was executed by firing squad at Bagumbayan" (Ocampo, 1990; Guerrero, 1963). "He faced his death with remarkable composure, turning to face the sky as he fell" (Guerrero, 1963; Ocampo, 1990).
IV

Life Experiences

The transformative journeys and pivotal moments that defined Rizal

Experiences Abroad+

Rizal's decade abroad (1882–1892) fundamentally transformed him from a bright student into a world-class intellectual and reformist leader (Quibuyen, 1997; Guerrero, 1963).


Spain (1882–1885, 1890–1891): Studied medicine and philosophy at Universidad Central de Madrid. Became active in the Propaganda Movement, writing for La Solidaridad under pen names "Laong Laan" and "Dimasalang" (Ocampo, 2011). "Filipinas dentro de Cien Años" was first published in the Filipino review La Solidaridad from September 1889 to January 1890 (Ocampo, 2011).


France (Paris, 1885, 1889): Worked as assistant to Dr. Louis de Wecker, a leading French ophthalmologist. Annotated Antonio Morga's Sucesos de las Islas Filipinas at the Bibliothèque Nationale—"an expression of the writing of the History of the Philippines from a Filipino viewpoint" (Ocampo, 2011, p. 3).


Germany (Heidelberg, Berlin, Leipzig, 1886–1887): Completed his specialization in ophthalmology. Published Noli Me Tangere in Berlin in 1887 (Ocampo, 2011). Befriended scholars like Ferdinand Blumentritt (Guerrero, 1963).


Japan (1888): Fell in love with O-Sei-San (Seiko Usui), who taught him the Japanese language (Guerrero, 1963). He "chose duty over love" and left for America (Quibuyen, 1997).


United States (1888): Visited San Francisco, Oakland, Sacramento, and other cities. Was quarantined in San Francisco due to a cholera scare, and was disturbed by racial discrimination against African Americans and Chinese immigrants (Guerrero, 1963; Quibuyen, 1997).

🏠 Life in Dapitan (1892–1896)+

Rizal's exile in Dapitan, Mindanao, from July 17, 1892, to July 31, 1896, demonstrated his capacity for "progressive education, social entrepreneurship, and community development" (Quibuyen, n.d.).


Medical Practice: Operated a clinic where he treated patients from all walks of life, often without charge. He performed successful eye surgeries, including one on his own mother (Quibuyen, n.d.; Guerrero, 1963).


Education: Established a school for boys emphasizing academics, physical fitness, agriculture, and vocational skills. "No tuition was required; instead, students helped maintain the school's garden and grounds" (Quibuyen, n.d.).


Scientific Research: Collected and studied flora and fauna, sending specimens to European scientists. Several species were named after him: Draco rizali, Apogonia rizali, and Rhacophorus rizali (Quibuyen, n.d.).


Community Development: Built a waterworks system, constructed a relief map of Mindanao, improved the town's drainage, and lit the streets with coconut oil lamps (Quibuyen, n.d.).


Personal Life: Met Josephine Bracken, an Irish woman who became his companion. They had a son who died shortly after birth (Guerrero, 1963; Quibuyen, n.d.).

⚖️ Arrest, Trial, and Execution+

The Arrest: Rizal left Dapitan on July 31, 1896, volunteering as a doctor in Cuba (Guerrero, 1963). "The Katipunan's revolution broke out on August 26, 1896, and Rizal was implicated despite having no involvement" (Agoncillo & Guerrero, 1979; Quibuyen, 1997).


Imprisonment: Held in Fort Santiago from November 3 to December 29, 1896. During this time, he wrote his farewell poem "Mi Último Adiós" (Guerrero, 1963; Ocampo, 1990).


The Trial: A court-martial tried Rizal on charges of rebellion, sedition, and illegal association. "The prosecution relied on flimsy evidence—testimonies extracted under torture and guilt by association with the Katipunan (which had named him honorary president without his consent)" (Guerrero, 1963; Agoncillo & Guerrero, 1979).


The Execution: "On December 30, 1896, at 7:03 AM, Rizal walked from Fort Santiago to Bagumbayan field" (Guerrero, 1963; Ocampo, 1990). "As the shots rang out, he twisted his body to fall facing upward, toward the sky—a final gesture of defiance and love for his homeland" (Guerrero, 1963; Ocampo, 1990). He was 35 years old.


Aftermath: "His body was secretly buried in an unmarked grave at Paco Cemetery. His remains were later exhumed and now rest beneath the Rizal Monument at Luneta (Rizal Park), Manila" (Guerrero, 1963; Agoncillo & Guerrero, 1979).

V

Character Analysis

Examining Rizal's virtues, shortcomings, and the evolution of his character

✦ Virtues & Strengths

  • Intellectual Brilliance: Polymath who mastered over 22 languages, excelled in medicine, literature, art, and science (Guerrero, 1963; Ocampo, 2011). "His two novels remain masterpieces of world literature" (Ocampo, 2011).
  • Courage & Conviction: Returned to the Philippines despite knowing the danger, standing by his principles rather than choosing a safe life abroad (Quibuyen, 1997; Guerrero, 1963).
  • Selflessness & Sacrifice: "Gave up personal comfort, love, and career opportunities abroad to serve his countrymen" (Guerrero, 1963; Quibuyen, 1997).
  • Peaceful Reformism: "A man with such morale as Jose Rizal was chosen by the Americans as the Philippines' national hero because of his diplomatic way of battling the Spaniards" (Ocampo, 2011, p. 2). He advocated for change through education and writing rather than violence.
  • Industriousness: Even in exile, he remained productive—building, teaching, healing, and creating (Quibuyen, n.d.).
  • Love of Family: Deeply devoted to his parents and siblings, supporting them financially and emotionally throughout his life (Guerrero, 1963).
  • Composure in Death: Faced execution with extraordinary calm, "demonstrating the strength of character that defined his entire life" (Guerrero, 1963; Ocampo, 1990).
  • Cultural Pride: His annotation of Morga's work "is an expression of the writing of the History of the Philippines from a Filipino viewpoint" (Ocampo, 2011, p. 3).

✦ Shortcomings & Weaknesses

  • Elitist Tendencies: His reform vision reflected the perspective of the educated class (ilustrados), occasionally underestimating the capacity of the masses (Constantino, 1971; Quibuyen, 1997).
  • Over-reliance on Reason: His intellectual approach to reform "sometimes lacked the emotional urgency that mobilizes people" (Constantino, 1971).
  • Hesitation toward Revolution: "Because Rizal was such a pacifist, some Filipinos consider him to be a weak character, which made Bonifacio stronger. Bonifacio believed that Rizal's beliefs were not strong enough to battle such an oppressing regime like the Spaniards had become that he even called him a coward behind his back" (Ocampo, 2011, p. 2).
  • Romantic Idealism: His relationships sometimes suffered from his prioritization of nationalist duty over personal happiness (Guerrero, 1963).
  • Occasional Arrogance: "Accounts suggest he could be condescending toward those he perceived as intellectually inferior" (Ocampo, 1990; Quibuyen, 1997).
  • Religious Ambivalence: His relationship with the Church was complex—critical of friar abuses yet maintaining personal faith (Constantino, 1971; Guerrero, 1963).
  • Physical Limitations: His small stature and poor eyesight, though overcome through determination, sometimes limited his physical capabilities (Guerrero, 1963).

Evolution of Character Over Time

1861–1872: The Innocent Child

"A curious, gifted child nurtured by a loving family" in Calamba, where "the beauties of Calamba impressed him as a growing child and deeply influenced his mind and character" (Guerrero, 1963, p. 2).

1872–1882: The Awakening Student

The GOMBURZA execution and his mother's unjust imprisonment "awakened his consciousness" (Constantino, 1971; Agoncillo & Guerrero, 1979).

1882–1887: The Reformist Intellectual

Exposure to European liberalism transformed him into a vocal advocate for reform. He channeled his frustrations into the Noli Me Tangere (Ocampo, 2011).

1887–1892: The Cosmopolitan Patriot

World travels broadened his perspective. He wrote El Filibusterismo and led the Propaganda Movement (Quibuyen, 1997; Ocampo, 2011).

1892–1896: The Martyr-in-Waiting

Exile in Dapitan deepened his spirituality and connection to common Filipinos (Quibuyen, n.d.). His acceptance of likely death demonstrated his ultimate commitment to the nation (Guerrero, 1963).

Challenges & Obstacles Faced

  • Poverty in Europe: Despite his family's relative wealth in the Philippines, Rizal often lived in extreme poverty abroad (Guerrero, 1963; Ocampo, 1990).
  • Homesickness: Separated from family for over a decade, he suffered profound loneliness (Guerrero, 1963).
  • Family persecution: His family suffered for his activism—they were evicted from their lands, his brother Paciano was arrested and tortured (Agoncillo & Guerrero, 1979; Guerrero, 1963).
  • Failed romances: Lost his first love, Leonor Rivera; his love for O-Sei-San ended when he chose duty over comfort (Guerrero, 1963).
  • Political isolation: "Caught between the Spanish authorities who viewed him as a revolutionary and the Katipunan who found him too moderate" (Quibuyen, 1997; Constantino, 1971).
  • Health problems: Suffered from near-blindness, tuberculosis scares, and the physical toll of constant travel (Guerrero, 1963).
🏛️

How These Contributed to His Heroism

  • Suffering bred empathy: His personal experiences of poverty and injustice "deepened his understanding of the common Filipino's plight" (Quibuyen, 1997).
  • Exile proved his character: "Four years in Dapitan showed he could serve his community selflessly even without recognition—true heroism without audience" (Quibuyen, n.d.).
  • Family sacrifice fueled resolve: The persecution of his loved ones "confirmed that the colonial system was irredeemably unjust" (Agoncillo & Guerrero, 1979).
  • Personal loss universalized his love: Sacrificing personal happiness for national purpose "demonstrated that his patriotism was genuine, not merely intellectual" (Guerrero, 1963).
  • Death immortalized his message: His calm acceptance of martyrdom at age 35 "transformed his writings from reform proposals into sacred texts of a nation" (Ocampo, 2011; Constantino, 1971).
  • The pen proved mightier: "His novels did what armies could not—they awakened a sleeping nation and united Filipinos across class, region, and ethnicity" (Ocampo, 2011, p. 3).
VII

Conclusion

Reflecting on the legacy of José Rizal

Summary of Findings

José Rizal's character was the product of an extraordinary intersection of nature and nurture. His biological endowments—intellectual brilliance inherited from both parents, a resilient constitution despite physical limitations, and the diverse genetic heritage of Chinese, Malay, and Spanish ancestry (Panlasagui, 1999; Guerrero, 1963)—provided the raw material for greatness.


His environmental influences then shaped these gifts into instruments of national liberation: a nurturing family that valued education and justice (Guerrero, 1963); world-class schooling that exposed him to the best of human thought (Ocampo, 2011); the oppressive colonial context that demanded a response (Constantino, 1971; Agoncillo & Guerrero, 1979); and his transformative travels that showed him what freedom looked like (Quibuyen, 1997).


His life experiences—from the trauma of GOMBURZA and his mother's imprisonment, to the intellectual awakening in Europe, to the productive exile in Dapitan (Quibuyen, n.d.), to his ultimate martyrdom—each chapter added depth to his character and urgency to his mission.


Rizal was not a perfect man. He had elitist tendencies (Constantino, 1971), romantic missteps, and an ambivalent relationship with revolution (Quibuyen, 1997). Yet these very imperfections make his heroism more compelling—he was a real human being who chose, despite his fears and limitations, to stand for truth and justice even when it cost him everything (Guerrero, 1963).

"He who does not know how to love his own language is worse than a beast or a putrid fish... Why should we submit to tyranny when we can aspire to freedom?"
— José Rizal, from "Sa Aking Mga Kabata" (as cited in Guerrero, 1963, p. 1)

Reflection on Rizal's Legacy

Rizal's legacy transcends Philippine history. As UP Center for Integrative and Development Studies (n.d.) argues in "Rizal in the 21st Century: Relevance of His Ideas and Texts," he represents the universal idea that "intellectual courage and moral integrity can challenge even the most entrenched systems of oppression."


His two novels "continue to be studied not just as historical documents but as literary masterpieces that expose the mechanisms of power, corruption, and social control" (Ocampo, 2011, p. 3). "Despite the fact that today Rizal's works are read mainly in translations into English and Filipino, his privileged status as outstanding Filipino author results from the ease with which he wrote and his essential contributions to the birth of the nation" (Ocampo, 2011, p. 1).


As Ocampo (2011) observes, "Rizal wrote not only for his own generation, but also for future generations, anticipating that a more educated generation would value and comprehend his work." His emphasis on education as the key to national development remains the foundation of Philippine educational philosophy (UP CIDS, n.d.).
